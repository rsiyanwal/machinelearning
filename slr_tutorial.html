1 The Story: Why Regression?

In 1877, Sir Francis Galton noticed something curious while studying the heights of parents and their adult children. Tall parents tended to have tall children — but not as tall as themselves. Their heights "regressed" back toward the population average. He named this phenomenon regression, and the math he used to describe it became one of the most powerful tools in all of statistics.

Galton's Insight: "The height of children born to tall parents tends to move back, or 'regress,' towards the mean height of the population." Today we use regression to predict any dependent quantity from another.

Regression handles both. A direct relationship means Y rises as X rises. An inverse relationship means Y falls as X rises.

The word "Simple" means two variables only — one X, one Y. "Linear" means the relationship is captured by a straight line. Together: we fit a straight line to predict Y from X.

Regression is for prediction. Once we find the line, we plug in a new X value and read off the predicted Y. For example: "If we budget ₹8M for R&D next year, what profit should we expect?"

5 Least Squares Error — Why We Need a Formula

Any line has an intercept and slope. There are infinitely many possible lines. How do we find the best one? We measure how wrong each line is using the Sum of Squared Errors (SSE).

For each data point, compute the vertical distance from the actual Y to the predicted Ŷ on the line. Square it (to make it positive). Sum all squared distances.

SSE = Σ (Y − Ŷ)² = Σ (Y − a − bX)²
The line with the minimum SSE is the best fit.

6 The Formulas — Calculating a and b

Instead of trying millions of (a, b) combinations, the statistical approach gives us closed-form formulas that directly compute the optimal values:

b  =  ΣXY − n · X̄ · ȲΣX² − n · X̄²
a = Ȳ − b · X̄
where X̄ = mean of X values, Ȳ = mean of Y values, n = number of data points

8 Summary

Regression
Predicting one variable from another using a mathematical relationship.
Simple Linear Regression
Two variables (one X, one Y) fit by a straight line: Y = a + bX.
Dependent Variable (Y)
What we predict. Its value depends on X.
Independent Variable (X)
What we use as input. Its value is not caused by Y.
Y-Intercept (a)
Where the regression line crosses the y-axis (value of Y when X = 0).
Slope (b)
How much Y changes for each 1-unit increase in X. Positive = direct, Negative = inverse.
Least Squares
We choose a and b to minimize SSE = Σ(Y − Ŷ)². This gives the unique best-fit line.
Prediction
Plug any new X into Ŷ = a + bX. The output is our estimated Y value.
